ISSUE
------------------------------------------------------------

Whether Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, which prohibits employment discrimination based on sex, extends protection to employees who have experienced same-sex sexual harassment in the workplace, as established by the principles set forth in Burlington Industries, Inc. v. Ellerth (1998) and Pennsylvania State Police v. Suders (2004). Specifically, the issue at hand is whether the Supreme Court's rulings in these cases, which established that unwelcome sexual advances, requests for sexual favors, and other verbal or physical conduct of a sexual nature constitute sexual harassment when submission to or rejection of such conduct affects an individual's employment, also apply to same-sex harassment, and whether such harassment can lead to a claim of constructive discharge.

------------------------------------------------------------

Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 prohibits sexual harassment in the workplace, regardless of the sexes involved. The Supreme Court has established that unwelcome sexual advances, requests for sexual favors, and other verbal or physical conduct of a sexual nature constitute sexual harassment when submission to or rejection of such conduct affects an individual's employment (Burlington Industries, Inc. v. Ellerth, 524 U.S. 742, 1998). This principle also applies to same-sex harassment (Oncale v. Sundowner Offshore Services, Inc., 523 U.S. 75, 1998).

In Burlington Industries, Inc. v. Ellerth, the Court held that supervisors can be held liable for sexual harassment if they know or should have known of the harassment and fail to take prompt and appropriate corrective action. The Court also emphasized that victims of harassment do not have to suffer a tangible employment action, such as termination or demotion, to bring a claim (Burlington Industries, Inc. v. Ellerth, 524 U.S. 742, 1998).

In Pennsylvania State Police v. Suders, the Court clarified that a hostile work environment can lead to a claim of constructive discharge, which occurs when an employee is forced to resign due to intolerable working conditions (Pennsylvania State Police v. Suders, 542 U.S. 129, 2004).

In Meritor Savings Bank v. Vinson, the Court established that sexual harassment can include quid pro quo harassment, where submission to or rejection of sexual advances is used as a basis for employment decisions (Meritor Savings Bank v. Vinson, 477 U.S. 57, 1986).

------------------------------------------------------------

In the analysis of the issue at hand, it is crucial to apply the established principles from the RULE section to the specific facts of each case.

Here, in Burlington Industries, Inc. v. Ellerth, Kimberly Ellerth, a salesperson, alleged that her supervisor, Ted Slowik, made repeated sexually suggestive comments and touched her inappropriately. Ellerth's case falls under the Supreme Court's ruling that supervisors can be held liable for sexual harassment if they know or should have known of the harassment and fail to take prompt and appropriate corrective action (Burlington Industries, Inc. v. Ellerth, 524 U.S. 742, 1998).

In Pennsylvania State Police v. Suders, Nancy Drew Suders, a communications operator, alleged that several male supervisors subjected her to ongoing sexual harassment, including lewd comments and physical touching. The Supreme Court clarified in this case that a hostile work environment can lead to a claim of constructive discharge, which occurs when an employee is forced to resign due to intolerable working conditions (Pennsylvania State Police v. Suders, 542 U.S. 129, 2004).

Similarly to Suders, Mechelle Vinson in Meritor Savings Bank v. Vinson was subjected to sexual harassment by her supervisor, which included unwelcome sexual advances and retaliation for refusing these advances. The Court established in this case that sexual harassment can include quid pro quo harassment, where submission to or rejection of sexual advances is used as a basis for employment decisions (Meritor Savings Bank v. Vinson, 477 U.S. 57, 1986).

Joseph Oncale, in Oncale v. Sundowner Offshore Services, Inc., was subjected to repeated acts of sexual harassment by male co-workers. The Court ruled in this case that Title VII's prohibition on sexual harassment applies to same-sex harassment in the workplace (Oncale v. Sundowner Offshore Services, Inc., 523 U.S. 75, 1998).

Teresa Harris, in Harris v. Forklift Systems, Inc., alleged that the company’s president, Charles Hardy, subjected her to a hostile work environment by making repeated sexually harassing comments. The Court held in this case that for a hostile work environment to exist, the harassment must be severe or pervasive enough to create an objectively hostile or abusive work environment or to change the terms and conditions of employment (Harris v. In all these cases, the victims did not have to suffer a tangible employment action, such as termination or demotion, to bring a claim. The victims' experiences of sexual harassment, whether it be quid pro quo or a hostile work environment, affected their employment and created a hostile or abusive work environment.

The strongest counterargument to the application of Title VII to same-sex harassment is the argument that Title VII does not explicitly include sexual orientation as a protected characteristic. However, the Supreme Court's ruling in Oncale v. Sundowner Offshore Services, Inc. established that Title VII's prohibition on sexual harassment applies to same-sex harassment in the workplace, regardless of whether sexual orientation is explicitly mentioned (Oncale v. Sundowner Offshore Services, Inc., 523 U.S. 75, 1998).

In conclusion, Title VII protects employees from same-sex sexual harassment, and such harassment can lead to claims of constructive discharge. The harassment must meet the standards set by the Supreme Court, which require that the harassment be severe or pervasive enough to create an objectively hostile or abusive work environment or to change the terms and conditions of employment. The principles established in Burlington Industries, Inc. v. Ellerth, Pennsylvania State Police v. Suders, Meritor Savings Bank v. Vinson, Oncale v. Sundowner Offshore Services, Inc., and Harris v.
