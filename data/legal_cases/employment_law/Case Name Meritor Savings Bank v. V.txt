Case Name: Meritor Savings Bank v. Vinson
Citation: 477 U.S. 57
Court: Supreme Court of the United States
Year: 1986
Area: Employment Law (Title VII – Sexual Harassment)

---

FACTS:

Mechelle Vinson began working as a teller-trainee at Meritor Savings Bank in Washington, D.C. Over time, she was promoted to assistant branch manager. Vinson alleged that during her four years at the bank, her supervisor, Sidney Taylor, subjected her to repeated sexual harassment, including sexual intercourse on multiple occasions, fondling, exposure, and rape. She claimed that she feared losing her job if she refused his advances. Although there was no evidence that her employment benefits were directly conditioned on sexual favors, Vinson contended that the conduct created a hostile and abusive work environment.

Vinson eventually filed suit under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964, alleging sexual harassment constituting sex discrimination. The district court ruled in favor of the bank, holding that because Vinson had voluntarily engaged in the sexual relationship and suffered no tangible economic loss, there was no Title VII violation. The court also concluded that the bank was not liable because it lacked actual knowledge of the harassment.

The United States Court of Appeals for the D.C. Circuit reversed, holding that a hostile work environment claim was cognizable under Title VII and that the bank could be liable even absent direct knowledge. The Supreme Court granted certiorari to resolve whether sexual harassment that creates a hostile work environment violates Title VII and under what circumstances an employer may be held liable.

---

ISSUE:

Whether sexual harassment that creates a hostile or abusive work environment constitutes discrimination “because of sex” under Title VII, even when the victim suffers no tangible economic loss, and what standard governs employer liability for a supervisor’s conduct.

---

HOLDING:

The Supreme Court unanimously held that sexual harassment leading to a hostile or abusive work environment is a form of sex discrimination prohibited by Title VII, even in the absence of tangible economic injury. The Court vacated the judgment of the Court of Appeals and remanded the case for further proceedings. While recognizing hostile environment claims, the Court rejected automatic employer liability and directed lower courts to apply traditional agency principles in determining employer responsibility.

---

REASONING:

Justice Rehnquist, writing for the Court, emphasized that Title VII prohibits discrimination affecting the “terms, conditions, or privileges of employment.” The Court relied heavily on Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) guidelines recognizing sexual harassment as a violation of Title VII. It concluded that harassment need not result in economic harm to be actionable; psychological harm and an abusive working environment can also alter employment conditions.

The Court rejected the district court’s view that voluntary participation in sexual conduct automatically negates a harassment claim. The correct inquiry, the Court stated, is whether the conduct was unwelcome, not whether it was voluntary in a literal sense. A subordinate may submit to advances out of fear of retaliation, which does not equate to genuine consent.

The Court also clarified that not all workplace conduct of a sexual nature constitutes actionable harassment. To violate Title VII, the conduct must be sufficiently severe or pervasive to create an objectively hostile or abusive work environment.

Regarding employer liability, the Court declined to adopt strict liability for employers in all cases involving supervisory harassment. Instead, it held that traditional agency principles should guide the analysis. While EEOC guidelines suggested employer responsibility regardless of notice, the Court declined to treat those guidelines as controlling. It indicated that factors such as whether the employer knew or should have known of the harassment would be relevant.

The Court’s reasoning balanced competing policy considerations. Recognizing hostile work environment claims advanced Title VII’s broad remedial purpose, ensuring workplace equality and dignity. At the same time, reliance on agency principles prevented imposing automatic liability on employers without regard to fault or knowledge. The decision laid the foundation for later cases, such as Burlington Industries v. Ellerth and Faragher v. City of Boca Raton, which further refined employer liability standards.

---

LEGAL PRINCIPLES:

1. Hostile Work Environment Doctrine: Sexual harassment that is severe or pervasive enough to create an abusive work environment violates Title VII.
2. No Tangible Economic Loss Required: Psychological and environmental harms can alter “terms and conditions” of employment.
3. Unwelcomeness Standard: The key inquiry is whether the conduct was unwelcome, not whether the employee appeared to participate voluntarily.
4. Agency-Based Employer Liability: Employer responsibility is determined by traditional agency principles rather than automatic strict liability.

---

KEY QUOTES:

“Without question, when a supervisor sexually harasses a subordinate because of the subordinate’s sex, that supervisor ‘discriminate[s]’ on the basis of sex.”

“Title VII affords employees the right to work in an environment free from discriminatory intimidation, ridicule, and insult.”
