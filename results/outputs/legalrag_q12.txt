QUERY: What is the substantial performance doctrine and when does it allow recovery despite minor contract deviations?

ISSUE
------------------------------------------------------------

Whether, in the context of a construction contract as illustrated in Jacob & Youngs, Inc. v. Kent, the substantial performance doctrine allows for recovery by the contractor despite minor deviations from the contractual specifications, and if so, what damages, if any, are recoverable in light of the principles established in Hadley v. Baxendale.

============================================================

RULE
------------------------------------------------------------

In the context of a construction contract, as established in Jacob & Youngs, Inc. v. Kent (1921), the substantial performance doctrine allows for recovery by the contractor despite minor deviations from the contractual specifications. This doctrine stipulates that a contractor may recover for work completed, even if there are minor defects or deviations, as long as the overall performance is substantially in accordance with the contract.

However, the damages recoverable in such cases are subject to the principles established in Hadley v. Baxendale (1854). According to Hadley v. Baxendale, damages must be either foreseeable at the time of contract formation (direct damages) or damages that would naturally arise in the ordinary course of things from a breach of contract (consequential damages).

In the case of Jacob & Youngs, Inc. v. Kent, the court held that the contractor was entitled to recover the contract price minus the cost of correcting the deviations, as these deviations were not foreseeable at the time of contract formation.

It is important to note that any deviations or defects that materially affect the intended use or purpose of the construction project may be considered a failure to perform substantially in accordance with the contract, and may result in the contractor not being entitled to recovery.

In Raffles v. Wichelhaus (The “Peerless” Case) (1864), the court held that where there is a mutual mistake in a contract, neither party can recover damages for breach of contract, as there is no breach of a valid contract. This exception to the general rule of substantial performance applies when both parties are under a mistake as to a fundamental term of the contract.

Lastly, in Griggs v. Duke Power Co. (1971), the court held that employment practices that discriminate on the basis of race, color, religion, sex, or national origin are unlawful under Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964. This case is not directly related to the substantial performance doctrine, but it serves as an important reminder that contracts must comply with applicable laws and regulations.

In summary, in a construction contract, the substantial performance doctrine allows for recovery by the contractor despite minor deviations from the contractual specifications, as long as the overall performance is substantially in accordance with the contract. The damages recoverable are subject to the principles established in Hadley v. Baxendale, with exceptions for cases of mutual mistake (Raffles v. Wichelhaus) and discrimination (Griggs v. Duke Power Co.).
