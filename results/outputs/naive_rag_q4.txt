 Title: Analysis of Employer's Ability to Terminate an Employee to Prevent Pension Benefits from Vesting without Violating the Age Discrimination in Employment Act (ADEA)

I. Introduction

The question at hand is whether an employer can terminate an employee to prevent pension benefits from vesting without violating the Age Discrimination in Employment Act (ADEA). This memorandum will analyze the issue by examining relevant legal precedents and applying the principles established in these cases to the given scenario.

II. Relevant Legal Precedents

A. Hazen Paper Co. v. Biggins, 507 U.S. 604 (1993)

In Hazen Paper Co. v. Biggins, the Supreme Court of the United States held that an employer's termination of an employee shortly before the employee's pension benefits were scheduled to vest could potentially violate the ADEA if the termination was motivated by the employee's age.

B. Carlill v. Carbolic Smoke Ball Company, [1893] 1 Q.B. 256 (C.A.)

Carlill v. Carbolic Smoke Ball Company established the principle that a unilateral contract can be formed when an offeror makes an offer to the public and a member of the public accepts the offer by performing the required act.

C. Jacob & Youngs, Inc. v. Kent, 230 N.Y. 239, 129 N.E. 889 (N.Y. 1921)

Jacob & Youngs, Inc. v. Kent established the rule that substantial performance of a contract is sufficient to entitle a party to recover damages for any defects or omissions in the performance.

D. Lefkowitz v. Great Minneapolis Surplus Store, Inc., 251 Minn. 188, 86 N.W.2d 689 (Minn. 1957)

Lefkowitz v. Great Minneapolis Surplus Store, Inc. held that an advertisement offering specific items for sale on a "first come, first served" basis constitutes an offer that can be accepted by the first person who complies with the terms of the offer.

E. Frigaliment Importing Co. v. B.N.S. International Sales Corp., 190 F. Supp. 116 (S.D.N.Y. 1960)

Frigaliment Importing Co. v. B.N.S. International Sales Corp. established that ambiguities in a contract should be construed against the party who drafted the contract and that trade usages may be considered in interpreting the contract.

III. Analysis

A. Application of Hazen Paper Co. v. Biggins

In the given scenario, an employer terminated an employee shortly before the employee's pension benefits were scheduled to vest. The employer's action could potentially violate the ADEA if the termination was motivated by the employee's age, as established in Hazen Paper Co. v. Biggins. To determine whether the termination was age-based, the court would need to consider evidence of age-related comments or actions by the employer, disparate treatment of older employees, or statistical evidence of age discrimination.

B. Potential Contractual Implications

If the employee's employment agreement included a provision regarding pension vesting, the termination could also have contractual implications. The principles established in Jacob & Youngs, Inc. v. Kent and Frigaliment Importing Co. v. B.N.S. International Sales Corp. suggest that the employer may be required to pay damages for any defects or omissions in the performance of the contract, including the failure to allow the pension benefits to vest.

C. Age Discrimination Claim under ADEA

If the termination was found to be age-based, the employee could potentially bring a claim under the ADEA. The ADEA prohibits discrimination on the basis of age in employment, including hiring, firing, and other terms and conditions of employment. To establish a claim, the employee would need to prove that: (1) they are 40 years of age or older; (2) they were qualified for the position; (3) they suffered an adverse employment action; and (4) their age was a determining factor in the adverse employment action.

D. Potential Contractual Remedies

If the termination was found to be in breach of contract, the employee could potentially recover damages for the loss of pension benefits. The principles established in Jacob & Youngs, Inc. v. Kent suggest that substantial performance of the contract is sufficient to entitle the employee to recover damages for any defects or omissions in the performance.

IV. Conclusion

In conclusion, an employer's termination of an employee to prevent pension benefits from vesting could potentially violate the ADEA if the termination was motivated by the employee's age. The court would need to consider evidence of age-related comments or actions by the employer, disparate treatment of older employees, or statistical evidence of age discrimination to determine whether the termination was age-based. If the termination was found to be in breach of contract, the employee could potentially recover damages for the loss of pension benefits. Employers should exercise caution when considering terminations that may impact an employee's pension benefits to avoid potential claims under the ADEA and breach of contract.
