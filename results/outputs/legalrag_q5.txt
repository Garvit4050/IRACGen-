QUERY: What must an employee prove to establish age discrimination when the employer provides a legitimate reason for termination?

============================================================

ISSUE
------------------------------------------------------------

In the context of employment law, specifically the Age Discrimination in Employment Act (ADEA), the issue at hand is whether an employee, such as Percy Green (McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green, 411 U.S. 792, 1973) or William Biggins (Hazen Paper Co. v. Biggins, 507 U.S. 604, 1993), must prove that age discrimination was the motivating factor in their termination, even when the employer provides a legitimate reason for the termination. The question is framed within the burden-shifting framework established by the McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green case, where an employee initially establishes a prima facie case of discrimination, and the employer then has the opportunity to offer a legitimate non-discriminatory reason for the termination. The issue revolves around the standard that the employee must meet to establish age discrimination when the employer provides a legitimate reason for termination.

RULE
------------------------------------------------------------

In the context of employment law, specifically the Age Discrimination in Employment Act (ADEA), the following rules apply:

1. McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green (1973) established a burden-shifting framework for proving discrimination. Initially, an employee must establish a prima facie case of discrimination by demonstrating that they belong to a protected class, were qualified for the job, suffered an adverse employment action, and that the position was filled by a person outside the protected class or the employee was treated less favorably.

2. In Hazen Paper Co. v. Biggins (1993), the Supreme Court held that an employee who establishes a prima facie case under the McDonnell Douglas framework may, upon the employer's rebuttal, still have the burden of proving that age discrimination was the motivating factor in the adverse employment action.

3. Reeves v. Sanderson Plumbing Products, Inc. (2000) clarified that the burden of proof shifts back to the employer once the employee has presented a prima facie case. The employer must then articulate a legitimate, non-discriminatory reason for the adverse employment action. If the employer meets this burden, the burden shifts back to the employee to prove that the employer's stated reason is a pretext for discrimination.

4. Burlington Industries, Inc. v. Ellerth (1998) and Oncale v. Sundowner Offshore Services, Inc. (1998) are relevant in the context of sexual harassment under Title VII. Ellerth established that employers can be held liable for sexual harassment by a supervisor, while Oncale extended this liability to same-sex harassment in hostile work environment cases.

Exceptions and Limitations:
The burden of proof may shift differently depending on the specific circumstances of each case, and the above rules may not apply in cases where the employer can demonstrate that the adverse employment action was based on a factor other than discrimination. Additionally, these rules may not apply in cases where the employee cannot establish a prima facie case, or where the employer can demonstrate that the adverse employment action was based on a legitimate, non-discriminatory reason.
