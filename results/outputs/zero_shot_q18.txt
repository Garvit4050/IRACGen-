 Title: Market Share Liability in Defective Products from Multiple Manufacturers

I. Introduction

This legal memorandum aims to analyze the concept of market share liability and its applicability to defective products from multiple manufacturers. Market share liability is a theory of liability that allows a plaintiff to recover damages from manufacturers whose combined market share exceeds a certain percentage, even when the specific manufacturer of the defective product cannot be identified.

II. Issue

The primary issue to be addressed is the legal principle of market share liability, its application in product liability cases involving multiple manufacturers, and the conditions under which it can be invoked.

III. Rule

1. The general rule in product liability cases is that a plaintiff must prove that a specific manufacturer is responsible for the defective product that caused their injury or damage. (Restatement (Third) of Torts: Products Liability § 2, 1998)

2. However, in cases where the identity of the manufacturer of the defective product is uncertain, the doctrine of market share liability may be invoked. (Philip Morris USA v. Williams, 549 U.S. 346, 355 (2007))

3. Market share liability is applicable when the plaintiff cannot identify the specific manufacturer of the defective product and it is impracticable to test each product from each manufacturer to determine the source of the defect. (Sindell v. Abbott Laboratories, 26 Cal. 3d 588, 607 (1979))

IV. Analysis

1. The doctrine of market share liability has its roots in the case of Sindell v. Abbott Laboratories, where the California Supreme Court held that a plaintiff could recover damages from manufacturers whose combined market share exceeded 50% during the relevant time period, even when the specific manufacturer of the defective product could not be identified. (Sindell v. Abbott Laboratories, 26 Cal. 3d 588, 607 (1979))

2. The application of market share liability in product liability cases has been debated extensively. Critics argue that it violates the principle of strict liability by shifting the burden of proof from the plaintiff to the defendants. (Restatement (Third) of Torts: Products Liability § 28C, 1998)

3. However, proponents of market share liability argue that it is a fair and practical solution in cases where the identity of the manufacturer is uncertain and the costs of testing each product from each manufacturer would be prohibitive. (Philip Morris USA v. Williams, 549 U.S. 346, 355 (2007))

4. The Supreme Court of the United States has addressed the issue of market share liability in the case of Philip Morris USA v. Williams. The Court held that market share liability could be used in tobacco litigation, but only under specific circumstances and with certain safeguards to protect the rights of the defendants. (Philip Morris USA v. Williams, 549 U.S. 346, 355 (2007))

V. Conclusion

In conclusion, market share liability is a theory of liability that allows a plaintiff to recover damages from manufacturers whose combined market share exceeds a certain percentage, even when the specific manufacturer of the defective product cannot be identified. The applicability of this doctrine is subject to debate, with critics arguing that it violates the principle of strict liability by shifting the burden of proof from the plaintiff to the defendants. However, in certain circumstances, such as when the identity of the manufacturer is uncertain and the costs of testing each product from each manufacturer would be prohibitive, market share liability can be a fair and practical solution. The Supreme Court of the United States has recognized the use of market share liability in certain cases, but with specific safeguards to protect the rights of the defendants.
