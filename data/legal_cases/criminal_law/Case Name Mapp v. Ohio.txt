Case Name: Mapp v. Ohio
Citation: 367 U.S. 643 (1961)
Court: Supreme Court of the United States
Year: 1961
Area: Criminal Law (Fourth Amendment – Exclusionary Rule / Search and Seizure)

---

FACTS:

Police officers in Cleveland, Ohio, went to the home of Dollree Mapp searching for a bombing suspect and gambling equipment. The officers did not have a valid search warrant. When Mapp refused to allow entry without a warrant, officers forcibly entered her home. They later claimed to possess a warrant but failed to produce one at trial, and none was ever introduced into evidence.

During the search, police did not find the bombing suspect but discovered allegedly obscene materials in a trunk located in Mapp’s basement. She was charged and convicted under Ohio law for possession of obscene materials.

At trial, Mapp argued that the evidence had been obtained through an unconstitutional search and seizure in violation of the Fourth Amendment. However, under then-existing precedent, the exclusionary rule—which barred illegally obtained evidence—applied only to federal prosecutions, not to state courts. The Ohio courts affirmed her conviction.

Mapp appealed to the United States Supreme Court, asking whether evidence obtained in violation of the Fourth Amendment is admissible in state criminal prosecutions.

---

ISSUE:

Whether the exclusionary rule, which prohibits the use of evidence obtained in violation of the Fourth Amendment, applies to state criminal prosecutions through the Fourteenth Amendment.

---

HOLDING:

The Supreme Court held that the exclusionary rule applies to state courts through the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. Evidence obtained by searches and seizures in violation of the Constitution is inadmissible in state criminal trials. The Court reversed Mapp’s conviction. The decision was 6–3.

---

REASONING:

Justice Tom C. Clark delivered the majority opinion. The Court examined its earlier decision in Weeks v. United States, which established the exclusionary rule in federal cases. However, in Wolf v. Colorado, the Court had previously declined to apply the exclusionary rule to the states, although it recognized that the Fourth Amendment itself was enforceable against them.

The Court in Mapp reconsidered Wolf and concluded that without the exclusionary rule, the Fourth Amendment’s protections would be meaningless. If illegally obtained evidence were admissible, law enforcement would have little incentive to respect constitutional limitations.

The Court reasoned that the exclusionary rule is an essential part of the Fourth Amendment’s guarantee against unreasonable searches and seizures. It emphasized judicial integrity and deterrence of police misconduct. Allowing unconstitutional evidence in state courts would encourage violations and undermine public confidence in the justice system.

The Court incorporated the exclusionary rule against the states through the Fourteenth Amendment’s Due Process Clause. This decision significantly expanded federal constitutional protections in state criminal proceedings.

Justice Harlan dissented, arguing that the Court had exceeded its authority by overturning precedent and imposing federal standards on state criminal procedures.

Mapp v. Ohio became a landmark case in constitutional criminal procedure, ensuring that state law enforcement agencies are subject to the same exclusionary principles as federal authorities.

---

LEGAL PRINCIPLES:

1. Exclusionary Rule Application to States: Evidence obtained in violation of the Fourth Amendment is inadmissible in state courts.
2. Incorporation Doctrine: The Fourth Amendment is enforceable against the states through the Fourteenth Amendment.
3. Deterrence of Police Misconduct: The exclusionary rule exists to discourage unlawful searches and seizures.
4. Judicial Integrity Principle: Courts must not admit evidence obtained through unconstitutional conduct.

---

KEY QUOTES:

“All evidence obtained by searches and seizures in violation of the Constitution is, by that same authority, inadmissible in a state court.”

“Nothing can destroy a government more quickly than its failure to observe its own laws.”
