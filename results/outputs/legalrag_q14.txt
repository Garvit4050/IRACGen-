Firstly, extrinsic evidence is considered to discern the parties' presumed intentions. This approach is particularly useful when the contract terms are unclear or ambiguous, and the parties' intent is not readily apparent from the contract itself.

Secondly, the principle of "officious bystander" may be applied to determine the meaning that would be agreed upon by reasonable persons in the same situation. This principle serves as a fallback when the parties' intent cannot be ascertained from the contract or extrinsic evidence.

However, the application of these principles may be subject to certain limitations. Similarly, unconscionable terms may be struck down, and courts may refuse to enforce contracts containing such terms.

Moreover, courts may imply terms to ensure business efficacy, provided that such terms are necessary to give the contract a meaningful and practical operation. However, the implication of terms is not without its own uncertainties, as it requires the court to speculate what terms the parties would have included had they considered the matter.

The court may also apply the principle of "officious bystander" as a fallback when the parties' intent cannot be ascertained from the contract or extrinsic evidence. However, the court's decision may be influenced by factors such as foreseeable damages, unconscionability, and the need to imply terms for business efficacy.
