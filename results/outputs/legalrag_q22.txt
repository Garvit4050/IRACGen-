QUERY: Under what circumstances can police conduct a stop and frisk without probable cause for arrest?
============================================================

LEGAL MEMORANDUM

============================================================

ISSUE
------------------------------------------------------------

Whether, under the Fourth Amendment's search and seizure provisions, a police officer may conduct a stop and frisk without probable cause for arrest, as established in the Terry v. Ohio (1968) case, where an officer has a reasonable suspicion that the individual is armed and dangerous, as opposed to the exclusionary rule context of Mapp v. Ohio (1961), where the focus is on the admissibility of evidence obtained through unlawful search and seizure.

============================================================

RULE
------------------------------------------------------------

Under the Fourth Amendment of the United States Constitution, the search and seizure provisions allow for a police officer to conduct a stop and frisk without probable cause for arrest, provided that the officer has a reasonable suspicion that the individual is armed and dangerous, as established in the case of Terry v. Ohio (1968). In Terry, the Supreme Court held that a limited stop and frisk is permissible when a police officer has a reasonable suspicion that criminal activity may be afoot, and the officer's safety or the safety of others is at risk.

However, it is important to note that the focus of the Fourth Amendment is on the constitutionality of the search and seizure, not on the admissibility of evidence obtained through such actions, as in the case of Mapp v. Ohio (1961). In Mapp, the Supreme Court established the exclusionary rule, which states that any evidence obtained through an unlawful search and seizure is inadmissible in court.

In the case of Miranda v. Arizona (1966), the Supreme Court held that before any custodial interrogation, law enforcement officers must inform the suspect of their rights, including the right to remain silent and the right to an attorney. This is known as the Miranda warning.

The use of deadly force by police officers during a seizure is governed by the case of Tennessee v. Garner (1985). In Garner, the Supreme Court held that the use of deadly force to prevent the escape of an unarmed, non-dangerous suspect is generally unreasonable, and officers should instead use non-deadly force whenever possible.

Finally, in Katz v. United States (1967), the Supreme Court established that the Fourth Amendment protects individuals from unreasonable searches and seizures, even if the search occurs in a public place. The Court held that a person has a reasonable expectation of privacy in a private conversation, and the government must obtain a warrant to listen in on such a conversation.

Exceptions and limitations to these rules may apply, and the specific facts of each case will determine whether a search or seizure is constitutional. It is essential for law enforcement officers to be aware of these rules and to follow them to ensure that their actions are lawful and protect the rights of individuals.
