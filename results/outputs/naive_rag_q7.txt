 Title: Analysis of Constructive Discharge as a Tangible Employment Action Preventing Employer's Affirmative Defense

I. Introduction

This legal memorandum aims to analyze when an employee's constructive discharge constitutes a tangible employment action that prevents the employer from raising an affirmative defense. The analysis will be guided by the principles derived from the following relevant legal precedents: Pennsylvania State Police v. Suders, 542 U.S. 129 (2004); Carlill v. Carbolic Smoke Ball Company, [1893] 1 Q.B. 256 (C.A.); Frigaliment Importing Co. v. B.N.S. International Sales Corp., 190 F. Supp. 116 (S.D.N.Y. 1960); Lefkowitz v. Great Minneapolis Surplus Store, Inc., 251 Minn. 188, 86 N.W.2d 689 (Minn. 1957); and Jacob & Youngs, Inc. v. Kent, 230 N.Y. 239, 129 N.E. 889 (N.Y. 1921).

II. Issue

The central issue in this analysis is whether an employee's constructive discharge can be considered a tangible employment action that prevents the employer from raising an affirmative defense.

III. Analysis

A. Definition of Constructive Discharge

Constructive discharge refers to a situation where an employee is forced to resign due to intolerable working conditions created by the employer, rather than a direct termination (Pennsylvania State Police v. Suders, 2004).

B. Tangible Employment Action

A tangible employment action is a significant change in employment status, such as hiring, firing, failure to promote, reassignment with significantly different responsibilities, or a decision causing a significant change in benefits (Pennsylvania State Police v. Suders, 2004).

C. Constructive Discharge as a Tangible Employment Action

The question at hand is whether constructive discharge can be considered a tangible employment action. The answer lies in the impact of the constructive discharge on the employee's employment status. If the constructive discharge results in a significant change in the employee's employment status, such as resignation leading to loss of employment, it can be considered a tangible employment action (Pennsylvania State Police v. Suders, 2004).

D. Implications for Employer's Affirmative Defense

If constructive discharge is deemed a tangible employment action, it could prevent the employer from raising an affirmative defense, such as in cases of discrimination, if the constructive discharge was a result of discriminatory actions by the employer (Pennsylvania State Police v. Suders, 2004).

IV. Case Analysis

A. Pennsylvania State Police v. Suders (2004)

In Suders, the Supreme Court of the United States held that a constructive discharge can constitute a tangible employment action in the context of Title VII sexual harassment claims. The Court found that Suders' resignation due to intolerable working conditions created by her supervisors' sexual harassment was a tangible employment action, which allowed her to proceed with her discrimination claim.

B. Carlill v. Carbolic Smoke Ball Company (1893)

Although not directly related to employment law, Carlill v. Carbolic Smoke Ball Company illustrates the principle of a unilateral contract, where one party makes an offer and the other party accepts by performing the required act. This principle could potentially be applied to constructive discharge cases, where the employee's resignation could be seen as acceptance of the employer's intolerable working conditions.

C. Frigaliment Importing Co. v. B.N.S. International Sales Corp. (1960)

Frigaliment Importing Co. deals with contract interpretation and ambiguity, which is not directly related to the question at hand. However, the principles of contract law could potentially be applied to constructive discharge cases, where the employer's actions could be interpreted as breaching an implied contract of fair employment.

D. Lefkowitz v. Great Minneapolis Surplus Store, Inc. (1957)

Lefkowitz discusses the enforceability of advertisements as contracts. In the context of constructive discharge, an employer's actions could potentially be seen as breaching an implied contract based on job advertisements promising a fair and safe work environment.

E. Jacob & Youngs, Inc. v. Kent (1921)

Jacob & Youngs deals with substantial performance and damages in contract law. While not directly related to constructive discharge, it underscores the importance of fulfilling contractual obligations, which could potentially be applied to the employer's obligation to provide a fair and safe work environment.

V. Conclusion

Based on the analysis of the relevant legal precedents, it can be concluded that constructive discharge can be considered a tangible employment action if it results in a significant change in the employee's employment status. This could potentially prevent the employer from raising an affirmative defense in cases of discrimination or other adverse employment actions. However, the specific application of these principles may vary depending on the jurisdiction and the facts of each case.
