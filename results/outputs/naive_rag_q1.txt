 Title: Liability of Employers for Unreported Supervisor Sexual Harassment: An Analysis of Relevant Legal Precedents

I. Introduction

This memorandum aims to analyze the question of whether an employer can be held liable for a supervisor's sexual harassment if the employee did not report it through company channels. The analysis will be based on five relevant legal precedents: Carlill v. Carbolic Smoke Ball Company (1893), Burlington Industries, Inc. v. Ellerth (1998), Meritor Savings Bank v. Vinson (1986), Pennsylvania State Police v. Suders (2004), and Jacob & Youngs, Inc. v. Kent (1921).

II. Analysis

A. Employer Liability for Sexual Harassment under Title VII (Burlington Industries, Inc. v. Ellerth, Meritor Savings Bank v. Vinson, Pennsylvania State Police v. Suders)

1. Burlington Industries, Inc. v. Ellerth (1998) established that an employer can be held liable for a supervisor's sexual harassment if the employer is negligent in controlling the work environment, even if the victim did not report the harassment.

2. Meritor Savings Bank v. Vinson (1986) further clarified that an employer is liable if it knows or should have known about the harassment and fails to take immediate and appropriate corrective action.

3. Pennsylvania State Police v. Suders (2004) reaffirmed that an employer can be held liable for a hostile work environment created by supervisors, even if the victim did not report the harassment, as long as the employer had actual or constructive knowledge of the harassment.

B. Implication of Contract Law Principles (Carlill v. Carbolic Smoke Ball Company, Jacob & Youngs, Inc. v. Kent)

1. Carlill v. Carbolic Smoke Ball Company (1893) established the principle of a unilateral contract, where an offeror can impose a duty on a recipient even without an express agreement, as long as the recipient acts in reliance on the offer.

2. Jacob & Youngs, Inc. v. Kent (1921) illustrated that a party who fails to perform a condition of a contract can still be liable if they have substantially performed their obligations.

C. Synthesis of Principles

The principles from contract law can be synthesized to suggest that an employer may have an implied duty to prevent sexual harassment, even if not explicitly stated in an employment contract. If an employee is subjected to sexual harassment by a supervisor, they may be considered to have acted in reliance on the employer's implied duty to provide a harassment-free work environment. If the employer fails to take appropriate corrective action, they may be liable for breaching this implied duty, similar to a breach of contract.

III. Conclusion

Based on the analysis of the provided legal precedents, it can be argued that an employer can potentially be held liable for a supervisor's sexual harassment, even if the employee did not report it through company channels. This is due to the employer's implied duty to provide a harassment-free work environment, as suggested by principles from contract law. However, the specific circumstances of each case will determine the employer's liability. It is advisable for employers to establish clear anti-harassment policies and procedures, and to take prompt and effective action when harassment is reported or reasonably known.
