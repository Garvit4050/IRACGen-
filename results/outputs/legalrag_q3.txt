QUERY: What standard must be met to prove a hostile work environment under Title VII?

ISSUE
------------------------------------------------------------

Whether, in a Title VII claim of a hostile work environment, the plaintiff must demonstrate that the alleged conduct was both severe and pervasive, as established in Harris v. Forklift Systems, Inc., and that the employer was deliberately indifferent to the hostile conduct, as established in Burlington Industries, Inc. v. Ellerth.

============================================================

RULE
------------------------------------------------------------

In a Title VII claim of a hostile work environment due to sex, as established by the Supreme Court of the United States, the plaintiff must demonstrate that the alleged conduct was both severe and pervasive, as well as that the employer was deliberately indifferent to the hostile conduct.

1. Harris v. Forklift Systems, Inc. (1993) established that for a hostile work environment claim to succeed, the conduct must be severe or pervasive enough to create a work environment that a reasonable person would consider hostile or abusive.

2. Burlington Industries, Inc. v. Ellerth (1998) further clarified that an employer can be held liable if it knew or should have known about the hostile conduct and failed to take prompt and effective remedial action.

3. Meritor Savings Bank v. Vinson (1986) set a precedent that unwelcome sexual advances, requests for sexual favors, and other verbal or physical conduct of a sexual nature constitute sexual harassment when submission to or rejection of this conduct explicitly or implicitly affects an individual's employment decisions.

4. Pennsylvania State Police v. Suders (2004) expanded the definition of a hostile work environment to include same-sex harassment, as established in Oncale v. Sundowner Offshore Services, Inc. (1998). In Oncale, the Court held that Title VII's prohibition on sex discrimination encompasses same-sex harassment when the conduct is motivated by sex-based hostility.

Exceptions or limitations: The specific facts and circumstances of each case will determine whether the conduct is severe or pervasive enough to create a hostile work environment and whether the employer was deliberately indifferent to the hostile conduct. Additionally, the standard for liability may differ for supervisors and non-supervisory employees.

Here, in Harris v. Forklift Systems, Inc. (1993), the plaintiff, Teresa Harris, alleged that the company’s president, Charles Hardy, subjected her to a hostile work environment by making repeated sexually harassing comments and threats. The Supreme Court held that for a hostile work environment claim to succeed, the conduct must be severe or pervasive enough to create a work environment that a reasonable person would consider hostile or abusive. In this case, Harris' allegations of repeated sexually harassing comments and threats by her supervisor could potentially meet the severity requirement. In this case, it is unclear whether Burlington was aware of the harassment and, if so, whether it took adequate steps to address it.

In Meritor Savings Bank v. The Court set a precedent that unwelcome sexual advances, requests for sexual favors, and other verbal or physical conduct of a sexual nature constitute sexual harassment when submission to or rejection of this conduct explicitly or implicitly affects an individual's employment decisions. In this case, it appears that Vinson's supervisor's conduct could potentially meet the definition of sexual harassment.

In Suders, the plaintiff, Nancy Drew Suders, alleged that several male supervisors subjected her to ongoing sexual harassment, including lewd comments and physical touching. In Oncale, the plaintiff, Joseph Oncale, was subjected to repeated acts of sexual harassment by male co-workers. Both cases demonstrate that same-sex harassment can create a hostile work environment in violation of Title VII.
