QUERY: What warnings must police give to suspects before custodial interrogation and what happens if they fail to do so?
============================================================

LEGAL MEMORANDUM

============================================================

ISSUE
------------------------------------------------------------

Whether, during custodial interrogation, law enforcement officers in the United States are obligated to inform suspects of their Fifth Amendment rights, as established in Miranda v. Arizona (1966), prior to initiating questioning, and whether failure to do so may result in the exclusion of any statements obtained as a consequence, as per the Exclusionary Rule outlined in Mapp v. Ohio (1961).

============================================================

RULE
------------------------------------------------------------

In the United States, during custodial interrogation, law enforcement officers are obligated to inform suspects of their Fifth Amendment rights, as established in Miranda v. Arizona (1966). The Miranda ruling mandates that prior to initiating questioning, officers must inform suspects of their right to remain silent, their right to an attorney, and that anything they say can be used against them in a court of law. Failure to do so may result in the exclusion of any statements obtained as a consequence, as per the Exclusionary Rule outlined in Mapp v. Ohio (1961).

The Exclusionary Rule, as outlined in Mapp v. Ohio, states that evidence obtained in violation of a person's Fourth Amendment rights against unreasonable searches and seizures is inadmissible in a court of law. This rule applies not only to physical evidence but also to statements obtained as a result of an illegal search or seizure.

However, it is important to note that the Miranda warnings are not required in non-custodial situations, during routine investigatory stops, or when evidence is obtained independently of the interrogation. The Supreme Court has further clarified these exceptions in cases such as Terry v. Ohio (1968) and Tennessee v. Garner (1985).

In Katz v. United States (1967), the Supreme Court expanded the definition of a search under the Fourth Amendment to include electronic surveillance, stating that a person has a reasonable expectation of privacy in a location where they have a subjective expectation of privacy that society is willing to recognize as reasonable.

In summary, during custodial interrogation, law enforcement officers must provide Miranda warnings, and any statements obtained without these warnings may be excluded under the Exclusionary Rule. However, there are exceptions to this rule, and the protection of privacy extends beyond physical searches to include electronic surveillance.

------------------------------------------------------------

Arizona (1966), plays a significant role in safeguarding the rights of suspects during custodial interrogation. Here, Ernesto Miranda was arrested and interrogated without being informed of his Fifth Amendment rights. In this case, the Supreme Court held that the confession obtained during the interrogation was inadmissible due to the failure to provide Miranda warnings.

Similarly to Miranda, Mapp v. Ohio (1961) also revolves around the protection of constitutional rights. Dollree Mapp's home was searched without a valid warrant, and evidence obtained was inadmissible in court due to the Exclusionary Rule. The Exclusionary Rule, as outlined in Mapp, states that evidence obtained in violation of the Fourth Amendment rights against unreasonable searches and seizures is inadmissible in a court of law.

However, it is crucial to distinguish between custodial interrogations and other circumstances. For instance, in Terry v. Ohio (1968), the Supreme Court ruled that a brief investigatory stop, without a warrant or probable cause for an arrest, is permissible if the officer has a reasonable suspicion that criminal activity is afoot. In this case, the Court did not require Miranda warnings, as the stop was not considered custodial interrogation.

Tennessee v. Garner (1985) further clarifies the use of deadly force during a seizure. In this case, the Court held that the use of deadly force to prevent the escape of a fleeing suspect is permissible only when the officer has probable cause to believe that the suspect poses a significant threat of death or serious physical injury to the officer or others.

In Katz v. United States (1967), the Supreme Court expanded the definition of a search under the Fourth Amendment to include electronic surveillance. Here, Charles Katz was wiretapped without a warrant, and the Court held that a person has a reasonable expectation of privacy in a location where they have a subjective expectation of privacy that society is willing to recognize as reasonable.

Applying these principles to the given facts, it is clear that Miranda warnings should have been provided to Ernesto Miranda during his custodial interrogation. The failure to do so resulted in the confession being inadmissible in court, as per the Miranda ruling and the Exclusionary Rule outlined in Mapp v. Ohio.

This argument is countered by the fact that the Miranda warnings serve to ensure that suspects are aware of their rights and can make informed decisions during interrogation, thereby promoting a fair and just criminal justice system.

In conclusion, during custodial interrogation, law enforcement officers are obligated to inform suspects of their Fifth Amendment rights, as established in Miranda v. Arizona (1966). Failure to do so may result in the exclusion of any statements obtained as a consequence, as per the Exclusionary Rule outlined in Mapp v. Ohio (1961). However, there are exceptions to this rule, and the protection of privacy extends beyond physical searches to include electronic surveillance, as established in Katz v. United States (1967). It is crucial to distinguish between custodial interrogations and other circumstances, such as Terry v. Ohio (1968) and Tennessee v. Garner (1985), to ensure the proper application of these principles.
