RULE

In contract law, a contract may be deemed void due to a mutual mistake regarding a material term, as established in the case of Raffles v. However, it is important to note that for a mutual mistake to invalidate a contract, the mistake must be regarding a term that is both material and fundamental. As demonstrated in Hadley v. Baxendale (1854) 9 Ex. 341, a mere difference in expectations or a failure to foresee certain consequences does not constitute a mutual mistake.

In the event of a mutual mistake, the contract is voidable, not void. This means that either party can choose to treat the contract as invalid, but the other party may still contest the invalidity. This principle is illustrated in Lucy v. Zehmer (1954) 196 Va. 493, where the Supreme Court of Virginia held that a contract is formed if a reasonable person would believe the offer was serious, regardless of whether one party claimed to be joking.

Exceptions to the rule of mutual mistake exist, such as in the case of Jacob & Youngs, Inc. v. Kent (1921) 230 N.Y. 239, 129 N.E. 889, where the Court of Appeals of New York held that a party who substantially performs a contract, even if there was a mutual mistake, is entitled to reasonable compensation for the work done.

(1960) 190 F. Supp. 116, where the United States District Court for the Southern District of New York held that where a contract is ambiguous, courts may consider industry-specific trade usages to interpret the contract.
